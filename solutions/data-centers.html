The problem

Site mobilization covers everything
except moving people.

Parking is at the perimeter by design
The interior of the campus belongs to laydown yards, crane pads, staging, and haul roads. Contractor parking gets pushed to the edge — which means the walk from the truck to the work face is a half mile or more, and it gets longer as the build expands.
Shift change is a bidirectional surge
Thousands of trades try to reach the gate at the same moment, in both directions, through a single badging chokepoint. The crew that clocks out late and the crew waiting to start are the same bottleneck — and it repeats every shift, every day of the build.
Walk time is billable time
Craft hours are the budget. Every minute a pipefitter or low-voltage electrician spends crossing the site is a minute billed against the job that produces no installed work — multiplied by the headcount, multiplied by every shift, across a schedule measured in months.
The trades you can't afford to lose feel it daily
Power specialists, cooling specialists, generator techs, and fire protection techs are heavily recruited on mission-critical work. When competing projects match the package, the daily experience becomes the tiebreaker — and a half-mile walk in February is a small, repeated argument for taking the other job.

The solution

A fixed route that follows
the construction sequence.

FlexTram runs as a system, not a dispatch service. Routes are cut from contractor parking to the badging gate, then out to the work faces, laydown yards, and muster points — with posted boarding locations and posted schedules so crews know where to stand and when the next vehicle arrives. Nobody calls anybody. Nobody waits on a radio.

At shift change, vehicles run continuous loops in both directions to clear the surge, then step down to a lighter interval through the day for material runs, inspections, and crews moving between buildings. The peak is the design case; the rest of the day rides the same infrastructure.

As the build progresses and the work faces move, the route gets re-cut to match the new sequence. The system follows the schedule instead of the schedule bending around a vehicle's turning radius. Independently turning axles handle gravel, compacted dirt, and the tight geometry between buildings and staging.

Vehicles stage onsite for the duration and demobilize at closeout — no paving, no permanent infrastructure, no construction of its own. Scale up as craft headcount ramps toward peak, scale back as trades roll off. And when the campus goes live, the same routes serve client tours, vendor escorts, and staff parking loops.

Common questions

Why do data center construction campuses need worker transportation?

Because the campus outgrew the walk. A hyperscale build can run several hundred acres with a peak craft workforce in the thousands, and contractor parking sits at the perimeter because the interior is consumed by laydown yards, crane pads, staging, and active haul roads. That leaves electricians, pipefitters, millwrights, and every other trade walking a half mile or more from their truck to the work face — twice a day minimum, plus breaks and material runs. Every minute of that walk is on the clock, and none of it is installed work.

How does onsite transit work on an active construction site?

As a fixed-route system, not a dispatch service. Routes are set from contractor parking to the badging gate, then to the work faces, laydown yards, and break or muster areas, with posted boarding points and posted schedules. At shift change, vehicles run continuous loops in both directions to clear the surge, then drop to a lighter interval through the day for material runs, inspections, and crews moving between buildings. As the build progresses and work faces shift, the route is re-cut to match the new sequence — the system follows the schedule rather than the schedule bending around the vehicles.

Can FlexTram operate on an active jobsite with gravel, dirt, and construction traffic?

Yes. FlexTram runs on asphalt, gravel, compacted dirt, and grass, and the independently turning axles let it navigate the tight, changing geometry of an active site — around laydown areas, between buildings, and along haul roads shared with equipment. Vehicles deploy in hours with no permanent infrastructure, no paving, and no construction of their own. They can be staged onsite for the duration of the build and demobilized at closeout, or scaled up and down as the craft headcount ramps.

Does onsite transit help with craft labor retention on mission-critical projects?

It is one of the few levers a general contractor controls that costs relatively little and is felt every single day. Specialty trades on mission-critical work — low-voltage electricians, power specialists, cooling specialists, generator technicians, fire protection technicians — are in short supply and heavily recruited, and when competing projects match the wage package, the daily lived experience becomes the tiebreaker. A half-mile walk in weather at the start and end of every shift is a small, repeated reason to take the other job. A ride is a small, repeated reason to stay.

Can the same system serve client tours and campus operations after the build?

Yes. The same fixed-route model that moves craft during construction serves prospective colocation customers, investors, regulators, and executives touring a live campus — where the distances between the entrance, the data halls, the substation, and the generator yard are too far to walk comfortably in business attire. It also covers ongoing operations: technicians moving between buildings, badged escorts for vendors, and staff parking loops. Open-air seating keeps a tour group together and lets guests actually see the campus, which an enclosed van does not.
