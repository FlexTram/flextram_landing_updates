The true cost
of the golf cart.

At first glance, the golf cart makes perfect sense. It's affordable, familiar, and requires no explanation. But default solutions have a way of hiding their true costs — and the golf cart is no exception. The problem isn't the cart itself. The problem is what happens when you scale it.

The acquisition trap

A single golf cart feels like a modest investment. A fleet of them feels like a reasonable operational decision. But fleets have a way of growing quietly, one cart at a time, until the number bears no relationship to the actual transportation need.

Here's why: without a systems-level approach to onsite transportation, the response to any capacity problem is almost always the same — add another cart. Vendor needs a vehicle? Add a cart. A department head requests one? Add a cart. A sponsor wants dedicated transport? Add a cart. Before long, the fleet has tripled and no one can clearly explain why.

We've seen this play out at scale. One client was operating over 1,300 golf carts at a single event. At an average acquisition or rental cost per unit, that number represents an enormous capital commitment — one that had crept up gradually and had never been interrogated seriously. When we sat down with them, nobody could produce a clear justification for the fleet size. It had simply grown to meet perceived demand, one cart at a time.

The utilization problem

Acquisition cost is only the beginning. The more revealing number is utilization — and in most golf cart fleets, it tells a damning story.

The average utilization rate on a six-seat golf cart in a large event environment is around 30%. That means for every ten seats being paid for, seven are empty. Across a fleet of any meaningful size, that's an enormous amount of capacity that exists on paper, costs money in practice, and delivers nothing in return.

Why is utilization so low? Because golf carts are dispatched, not scheduled. They respond to demand rather than anticipating it. That means they spend a significant portion of their operational life waiting — parked, idling, or moving with one or two occupants when they could be carrying six. In a scheduled, route-based system, vehicles are deployed against known demand patterns. Utilization climbs sharply because the system is designed around movement, not response.

The hidden cost stack

Beyond acquisition and utilization, there is a layer of costs that rarely appears on any budget line but is very much real.

Fuel and maintenance are the most obvious. In a large, untracked fleet, fuel consumption is essentially unauditable. Vehicles are operated by dozens of different people across a sprawling site, refueled informally, and returned in varying states of repair. Maintenance backlogs build up quietly. Breakdowns during events are not just a cost — they're a liability.

Damage and loss compound the picture. In environments where hundreds of people are operating vehicles with minimal oversight, collision rates are higher than operators typically acknowledge. Vehicles are scraped, bumped, and occasionally written off entirely. In extreme cases — and we have seen this — vehicles are stolen outright or driven off-site.

Labor is perhaps the most underestimated cost. Every cart needs an operator. In a large fleet, that means a significant labor commitment that scales directly with fleet size. Reduce the fleet through smarter utilization and you reduce the labor requirement proportionally.

When you add acquisition cost, wasted capacity, fuel leakage, maintenance, damage, and labor together, the true cost of a large golf cart fleet is typically two to three times what operators believe it to be. The per-ride cost, when calculated honestly, is often startling.

What the math actually looks like

A scheduled, route-based system with higher-capacity vehicles doesn't just move more people. It collapses that cost stack significantly. Fewer vehicles, higher utilization, predictable fuel consumption, less labor, and far less damage exposure add up to a fundamentally different economic picture — one where transportation becomes a manageable, forecastable line item rather than a sprawling, opaque one.

The golf cart isn't the enemy. But the assumption that more carts equals better transportation is one that costs operators far more than they realize. The first step is simply doing the math.
